<p>While inherently critical to today’s businesses that run on data, implementing and enforcing data security and privacy has never been straightforward. Between collecting different types of sensitive data and deploying unique architectures, organizations cannot adopt a one-size-fits-all solution, meaning that every security architecture is unique. With no absolutes around how to build security into the organization’s IT environment, nearly every organization’s program relies on a risk assessment that drives the controls.</p>
<p>The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) published its Risk Management Framework (RMF) to offer guidelines that organizations could use when working to understand their security and privacy posture. The RMF offers a disciplined, structured, and flexible approach that organizations can use to make efficient, cost-effective, risk management decisions.</p>
<p>When working to understand system level and risk management processes, organizations can use the NIST Risk Management Framework as a guide that helps establish responsibility and accountability for implemented controls.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>What is the NIST Risk Management Framework?</h2>
<p>NIST Special Publication 800-37, the NIST Risk Management Framework, seeks to help organizations achieve several specific security objectives:</p>
<ul>
<li>Link and communicate risk management processes and activities at the governance level.</li>
<li>Institutionalize critical risk management preparatory activities across all levels.</li>
<li>Align risk management to the <a href="https://graylog.org/post/centralized-log-management-and-nist-cybersecurity-framework/">NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)</a>.</li>
<li>Integrate privacy risk into holistic risk management processes.</li>
<li>Align lifecycle systems engineering into secure software and system development.</li>
<li>Integrate supply chain risk management (SCRM) to address various risks and development practices throughout the secure software development lifecycle (SDLC).</li>
<li>Enable organizations to select controls that complement baseline controls outlined in NIST Special Publication 800-53.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>What are the seven steps in the NIST RMF?</h2>
<p>To successfully execute the RMF, NIST outlines the following steps:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Prepare</strong>: Establish the context and priorities.</li>
<li><strong>Categorize</strong>: Assess the impact of a potential data loss event.</li>
<li><strong>Select</strong>: Identify the controls that mitigate risk to acceptable levels.</li>
<li><strong>Implement</strong>: Deploy and describe how the organization uses controls.</li>
<li><strong>Assess</strong>: Ensure that the organization implemented controls correctly and that they work as intended to produce the desired outcomes.</li>
<li><strong>Authorize</strong>: Approve the controls after determining that they reduce risk appropriately.</li>
<li><strong>Monitor</strong>: Continuously assess control effectiveness, document system and operational changes, conduct risk assessments, analyze impact, and report on security and privacy posture.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>What are NIST RMF tasks?</h2>
<p>The NIST RMF lists discrete tasks that organizations can take to complete each of the seven steps.</p>
<h3>Prepare</h3>
<p>During the Prepare step, an organization carries out the following essential activities that map to its mission and business processes:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Risk management roles:</strong> Define and assign risk management roles and responsibilities while ensuring proper separation of duties, avoiding conflicts of interest, and considering organizational structure and people’s expertise.</li>
<li><strong>Risk management strategy: </strong>Define risk tolerance and establish a strategy that guides consistent, enterprise-wide decisions about assessing, responding to, and monitoring security and privacy risk.</li>
<li><strong>Risk assessment: </strong>Aggregate system-level and enterprise-wide risk information to assess organizational risk by considering various factors, like operations, external connections, and supply chain risk.</li>
<li><strong>Tailored control baselines: </strong>Customize standard control sets to align with organizational risk, mission requirements, and operating environments while maintaining appropriate security and privacy protections.</li>
<li><strong>Common control identification: </strong>Identify, implement, and manage shared controls that all systems can inherit to improve consistency, reduce duplication, and streamline risk management.</li>
<li><strong>Impact-level prioritization: </strong>Prioritize systems within the same impact level for more detailed, risk-based decisions that map to mission criticality and business objectives.</li>
<li><strong>Continuous monitoring strategy: </strong>Define an organization-wide approach to continuously assess control effectiveness, maintain visibility into risk posture, and enable ongoing, real-time risk management.</li>
<li><strong>Mission or business focus</strong>: Identify the objectives that the system supports so that security and risk decisions align with organizational priorities and goals.</li>
<li><strong>System stakeholders</strong>: Identify individuals and groups who have an interest in the system’s life cycle to ensure effective communication, accountability, and alignment of security and privacy considerations.</li>
<li><strong>Asset identification</strong>: Identify the physical assets, like facilities and machines, and the non-physical assets, like digital information software, or business processes, that the organization must protect to support system operations and organizational objectives.</li>
<li><strong>Authorization boundary</strong>: Define system component and resource scope so that the organization understands its responsibility for protection, accountability, and risk management.</li>
<li><strong>Information types</strong>: Identify and document the different information categories that the system processes to support security classification, risk assessment, and protection requirements.</li>
<li><strong>Information life cycle</strong>: Document how each data type moves through the system from creation to disposal to support risk assessment, control selection, and protection.</li>
<li><strong>Risk assessment &#8211; system</strong>: Assess system-level security, privacy and supply chain risks by evaluating threats, vulnerabilities, and potential impacts to inform risk-based decisions across the system life cycle.</li>
<li><strong>Requirements definition</strong>: Define formal security and privacy requirements using mission needs, risk assessment, regulations, and stakeholder input when selecting controls and system protections across the lifecycle.</li>
<li><strong>Enterprise architecture</strong>: Determine how the system fits into the organization’s broader technical and security landscape to align architecture, risk strategy, and enterprise-wide protections.</li>
<li><strong>Requirements allocation</strong>: Distribute required protections across system components and shared environment for efficient and appropriate security and privacy measure implementations.</li>
<li><strong>System registration</strong>: Formally record the organization’s governance processes to ensure appropriate tracking, oversight, and integration into enterprise security and privacy management.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Categorize</h3>
<p>During the categorize step, the organization engages in the following tasks when considering the potential adverse impact that data and system confidentiality, integrity, or availability loss would have:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>System description</strong>: Provide a structured overview of the system’s key characteristics, components, and operating context to support security categorization and risk-informed decision-making.</li>
<li><strong>Security categorization</strong>: Determine how information loss might impact organizations operations, assets, and individuals to understand the system’s overall risk level when selecting controls.</li>
<li><strong>Security categorization and approval</strong>: Validate and formally approve the system’s risk categorization so it aligns with operational risk strategy, mission needs, and appropriate protection requirements.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Select</h3>
<p>During the Select step, organizations engage in the following tasks when selecting, tailoring and documenting the controls that help mitigate risk:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Control selection</strong>: Define and select the security and privacy controls that will protect systems and the operational environment and map to the organization’s risk, requirements, and policies.</li>
<li><strong>Control tailoring</strong>: Refine and customize selected security and privacy controls so they align with system context, risk assessment results, and organizational requirements.</li>
<li><strong>Control allocation</strong>: Assign controls to system components and operational environments while distinguishing between system-specific, hybrid, and inherited controls in line with architecture and risk requirements.</li>
<li><strong>Documentation of planned control implementations</strong>: Document how the organization plans to implement the selected security and privacy controls with the system and its environment to trace requirements, design decisions, and planned protection in system security and privacy plans.</li>
<li><strong>Continuous monitoring strategy &#8211; system</strong>: Define a system-level approach for ongoing control effectiveness assessments that map with organizational monitoring requirements to guide continuous authorization and risk visibility throughout the system lifecycle.</li>
<li><strong>Plan review and approval</strong>: Formally evaluate and approve the system’s security and privacy plans to confirm that the selected control implementations and risk decisions meet organizational requirements.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Implement</h3>
<p>During the Implement step, organizations engage in the following tasks to deploy controls and document baseline configurations:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Control implementation</strong>: Apply the approved security and privacy controls that the system plans and architecture define to ensure correct integration, configuration, and documentation within the operational environment.</li>
<li><strong>Update control implementation information</strong>: Update security and privacy plans so that reflect actual control states to ensure configuration and implementation details remain accurate as part of supporting effective assessment and change tracking.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Assess</h3>
<p>During the Assess step, organizations engage in the following tasks to determine whether controls implementations are correct and operate as intended to achieve desired security and privacy outcomes:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Assessor selection</strong>: Select qualified and appropriately independent assessors or assessment teams that have the necessary expertise for evaluating control effectiveness across the system and its environment.</li>
<li><strong>Assessment plan</strong>: Define and approve the procedures for establishing how to test and validate implemented control effective across the system and its operating environment.</li>
<li><strong>Control assessments</strong>: Use a structured assessment procedure that provides evidence when evaluating implemented controls to determine whether they are correctly applied, function as intended, and effectively meet security and privacy requirements.</li>
<li><strong>Assessment reports</strong>: Document assessor findings, evidence, and recommendations from control evaluations as part of authorization decisions and risk determination.</li>
<li><strong>Remediation actions</strong>: Use targeted corrective actions to address assessment findings about controls before reassessing and updating documentation to ensure control effectiveness and accurate risk posture.</li>
<li><strong>Plan of action and milestones (POAM)</strong>: Identify and document a timeline for addressing control deficiencies to reduce risk by prioritizing remediation based on assessment finding, outlined corrective actions, and resources.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Authorize</h3>
<p>During the Authorize step, organizations engage in the following tasks when senior management determines whether controls reduce security and privacy risks to an acceptable level:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Authorization package</strong>: Compile and present the integration plans, assessment results, and remediation tracking information to support the authorizing official’s ability to make an informed, risk-based decision.</li>
<li><strong>Risk analysis and determination</strong>: Evaluate and compare authorization packages against the organizational risk inputs to determine the system’s level of security and privacy risk when using common controls.</li>
<li><strong>Risk response</strong>: Track mitigation activities using formal remediation and planning cycles to ensure that actions address identified risk, including mitigation or acceptance.</li>
<li><strong>Authorization decision</strong>: Explicitly accept or reject the residual risk in a formal authorization decision that defines whether or not the system or common controls can operate under the specified terms and conditions.</li>
<li><strong>Authorization reporting</strong>: Communicate the authorization outcome and any significant control deficiencies to relevant stakeholders and record the system’s authorization status using organizational tracking and governance systems.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Monitor</h3>
<p>During the Monitor step, organizations engage in the following tasks to maintain ongoing situational awareness about the system&#8217;s security and privacy posture to support risk management decisions:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>System and environment changes</strong>: Identify and document security and privacy impact by tracking and analyzing system and operating environment changes, including technical, human, and physical ones.</li>
<li><strong>Ongoing assessments</strong>: Use a continuous monitoring strategy to evaluate and validate implemented and inherited controls’ effectiveness and update assessment evidence for making decisions about risk.</li>
<li><strong>Ongoing risk response</strong>: Use continuous monitoring to adjust security and privacy posture by implementing mitigation actions or accepting residual risk then update assessment records to reflect current conditions.</li>
<li><strong>Authorization package updates</strong>: Continuously incorporate monitoring results to keep authorization artifacts current by updating security and privacy plans, assessment reports, and remediation tracking.</li>
<li><strong>Security and privacy reporting</strong>: Consolidate monitoring outputs, assessment results, and remediation progress into structure posture reports so authorizing officials and stakeholders have ongoing visibility into the system’s security and privacy status.</li>
<li><strong>Ongoing authorization</strong>: Use current posture information and monitoring outputs to continuously evaluate system risk so that the organization can confirm whether to accept residual risk or choose to sustain, adjust, or revoke authorization as needed.</li>
<li><strong>System disposal</strong>: Decommission components, update inventories and documentation, and ensure security and privacy controls remain intact when retiring a system.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Best Practices for Monitoring and Continuously Assuring NIST RMF Controls Function as Intended</h2>
<p>While the NIST RMF offers a structured process for managing security and privacy risk, organizations can reduce time to audit readiness by implementing the following best practices.</p>
<h3>Centralize Security Event Collection and Correlation</h3>
<p>Using a security information and event management (SIEM) solution to centralize, normalize, and correlate logs enables organizations to monitor security and privacy-relevant events and support ongoing assessment of control effectiveness. A SIEM enables organizations to correlate data from:</p>
<ul>
<li>Operating systems.</li>
<li>Authentication systems.</li>
<li>Endpoints</li>
<li>Network devices.</li>
<li>Cloud services.</li>
<li>Security tools.</li>
</ul>
<p>By correlating this data, organizations can continuously monitor system and environment changes with security data that informs ongoing control assessment and risk determination.</p>
<h3>Implement Auditable Event Recording and Traceability</h3>
<p>Audit logs provide the verifiable security activity record documenting that the controls adequately respond to risk and comply with established security and privacy plans. Effective audit logging can help answer many of the questions that the DPDPA requires in a breach notification:</p>
<ul>
<li>What action occurred?</li>
<li>Who performed the action?</li>
<li>When did the action take place?</li>
<li>Where did the activity originate?</li>
<li>What system or resource did the activity impact?</li>
</ul>
<p>These capabilities collectively support the NIST RMF Monitor and ongoing authorization activities by enabling continuous assessment of control effectiveness, real-time risk determination, and timely risk response based on changes to the system and its operating environment.</p>
<h3>Monitor User Behavior and Privileged Activity</h3>
<p>Data breaches can be caused by external or internal threat actors. User behavior and activity monitoring enables organizations to detect policy violations or compromised accounts faster. By establishing baseline behavior and monitoring for deviations, organization can detect security incidents with insight into:</p>
<ul>
<li>Credential misuse.</li>
<li>Insider threats.</li>
<li>Abnormal access patterns.</li>
<li>Unauthorized configuration changes.</li>
</ul>
<p>Ongoing user and privileged account monitoring can detect anomalous behavior or identify potential control failures to inform continuously risk response and authorization decisions.</p>
<h3>Enrich and Correlate Data to Prioritize Risk</h3>
<p>By centralizing log data in a single solution, organizations can augment the raw logs with context. Correlating enriched log events allows teams to prioritize high-risk security alerts and reduce false positives, improving investigation speed and accuracy. Some examples of context that organizations should apply to their data include:</p>
<ul>
<li>User identity.</li>
<li>Geolocation</li>
<li>Asset classification.</li>
<li>Threat intelligence.</li>
<li>User and asset risk scores.</li>
</ul>
<p>Adding context to log data helps organizations align with ACSC mitigation strategies like identifying:</p>
<ul>
<li>Suspicious login behavior.</li>
<li>Lateral movement.</li>
<li>Malware execution.</li>
<li>Exploit attempts.</li>
</ul>
<p>With continuous monitoring and enriching telemetry, organizations strengthen control assessments by improving the quality and context of evidence used to evaluate control effectiveness, and informing authorization decisions by providing risk-prioritized insights into system behaviour and residual risk exposure.</p>
<h3>Build Relevant Dashboards to Automate Compliance Reporting</h3>
<p>Security and compliance teams often struggle to prove that controls function as intended. A SIEM enables these teams to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Generate compliance dashboards.</li>
<li>Create scheduled reports.</li>
<li>Visualize security trends.</li>
<li>Provide documentation to auditors.</li>
</ul>
<p>Teams can use these dashboards to build reports around ACSC mitigations like:</p>
<ul>
<li>Privileged account activity.</li>
<li>Patch status.</li>
<li>Authentication failures.</li>
<li>Endpoint protection alerts.</li>
</ul>
<p>With automated dashboards and reporting that provide ongoing visibility into control effectiveness, organizations have the data for structured evidence control that improve transparency and traceability of the security and privacy posture information that help stakeholders make risk-based authorization decisions.</p>
